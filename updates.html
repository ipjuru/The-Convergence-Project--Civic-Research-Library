Founding Post March 2026

The Convergence Project: What It Is, Why It Exists, and What We're Building Together

Here's the hard truth: where we are right now is not one person's fault. These crises were not accidental. They were manuFRACTURED — manufactured on the surface, fractured at the foundation, and handed to us piece by piece so we'd never see the whole shape of the thing. The whole shape is now visible. And you deserve to see it.

What This Is

The Convergence Project is a free, publicly available Civic Research Library built for everyday people. Not academics. Not politicians. Not think tanks with agendas. People. It houses interactive research decks pulling from real, publicly sourced data — covering water, energy, space weather, basic needs, systemic root causes, the war economy, the American family, and much more — with new decks added as the research grows. The goal is simple: give you the clearest, most honest picture of where we are as a nation, grounded in facts, history, science, and patterns. No spin. No agenda. Just receipts.

Why It Exists

Here's the hard truth: where we are right now is not one person's fault. It's not one party's fault. It's not one generation's fault. And it is absolutely not reducible to race, though race has been used — masterfully and deliberately — as a distraction from the larger machinery at work.

What we are living through is the result of decades of unchecked power, collective inattention, and systems that were sold to us as separate problems. The economy. The environment. Public health. Education. Housing. Food security. We were told these were different conversations happening in different rooms. They were never in different rooms. They were always the same room — and the door was locked from the outside.

These crises were not accidental. They were manuFRACTURED. Manufactured on the surface, fractured at the foundation, and handed to us piece by piece so we'd never see the whole shape of the thing.

The Convergence Project exists because the whole shape is now visible. And you deserve to see it.

This Is For the Youth First

Because everything else is secondary if we lose them to the noise.

They are growing up in the most data-saturated environment in human history and simultaneously being handed the least context for what any of it means. That is not an accident either.

For the home educators, the deep thinkers, the ones who read everything and got called crazy for connecting dots out loud — this is for you too. You were not wrong. You were early. And the young people in your lives deserve the receipts to back up what you already knew.

We got misled. They don't have to be.

How It Works and How You Can Use It

Each research deck is designed to be a standalone deep dive — readable in one sitting, sharable, and applicable. You don't need a degree to use this. You need curiosity and a willingness to look.

Students and educators — both traditional and home-based — can use the decks as real civic curriculum. Not textbook sanitized history. Actual data alongside actual events to build the kind of critical thinking this generation needs more than any before it.

Community organizations and advocates can use the pattern data to strengthen cases for local policy reform — with sources attached.

Business owners and entrepreneurs can use the economic and systems data to understand the larger forces shaping their markets, supply chains, and communities.

Government agencies and reform advocates can use this as a mirror — an honest, data-backed reflection of where policy has drifted from its original purpose, and where rebuilding is not only possible but necessary.

Old policies that once served as lifelines can be rebuilt. Not restored — rebuilt. Differently. Better. With the clarity that only comes from understanding how we got here. That is still possible. The data says so.

How This Was Built and Where It's Going

I built this with the help of AI as a research and development tool — specifically for synthesizing data, building the interactive decks, and helping me organize years of accumulated research into something accessible. I'll be transparent: my current AI subscription runs through May of this year. After that, I will need technical support to keep this growing.

That means I am actively looking for:

IT professionals, web developers, and engineers who want to volunteer their skills toward a civic project that matters.

Researchers and data analysts who can help verify, update, and expand the data decks over time.

Educators, organizers, and community leaders who want to collaborate on how this material gets used in real spaces.

If you have data to contribute, a correction to flag, or want to get involved in any capacity — email directly at

All submissions will be reviewed and sourced properly before anything is added to the library.

A full Source Bucket — all sourced materials and hyperlinked references organized by deck — is now live on this page. For my fact-checkers and skeptics: good. Scrutiny is welcome here. That's the whole point.

I also intend to create virtual and in-person spaces — structured conversations where we can sit with this data together, ask hard questions, and start building the kind of organized, informed response this moment is asking for. More on that as it develops.

The Invitation

I have been saying these things through poetry for years. The urgency you hear in my work — this is where it comes from. Now I'm handing you the source material.

This is not a political project. This is a clarity project. The data doesn't belong to any party. It belongs to all of us. And what we choose to do with it — that's still ours to decide.

We can change. The patterns say we've done it before. The question is whether we want to badly enough, and whether we're willing to look honestly at what we built in the first place.

The Convergence Project is a reminder that we can. And for the next generation — it's proof that someone saw it coming, told the truth about it, and left a light on.

We got misled. They don't have to be.

Save it. Share it. Use it.

What Was Added

Deck 14 -- Mental Health in America. The infrastructure collapsed decades before anyone called it a crisis. The data on what was dismantled, who got left behind, and what the gap actually costs.

Deck 15 -- American Finances. $1.28 trillion in credit card debt. Wages flat for 50 years. Payday loans at 391% APR. The broke blueprint, fully documented.

Deck 17 -- Gig Economy & Wage Theft. $50 billion stolen from workers every year -- no mugshot, no perp walk. The data on misclassification, platform extraction, and what the gig economy really costs the people inside it.

Deck 18 -- American Politics. A democracy that started with 6% voter eligibility. A government that now correlates with elite preferences, not public will. The Princeton study. The campaign finance numbers. The turnout data.

Deck 19 -- The Machinery of Power. Lobbyists. Revolving doors. Congressional stock trades. Regulatory capture. The system is not broken -- it is working exactly as designed for the people who designed it.

Deck 20 -- Identity. More rights won than any generation before. More anti-LGBTQ+ legislation filed than any year on record. 850+ bills in 2025 alone. The data on both sides of that reality.

Deck 22 -- The Food System. Four companies control 85% of beef processing. 47.9 million people still go hungry. 67% of children's calories come from ultra-processed food. The system is not failing -- it is designed this way.

Deck 24 -- Criminal Justice. 2 million incarcerated. More than any nation on earth, in raw numbers and per capita. $0.63 an hour in prison labor wages. An 83% rearrest rate without reentry support. Built to punish. The receipts are here.

A Note on How This Site Works

This library is built by one person, with AI as a research and development tool, in real time. That means it grows in batches. It means some things get updated before others. It means occasionally something looks off while a fix is in progress.

I am asking you to bear with me. Not because the work is not serious -- it is. But because this is a living document, not a finished product. Every deck you see is the result of hours of research, sourcing, writing, building, and checking. It will keep growing. The volumes are mapped. The roadmap is real. We are not done.

This library is free. It will stay free. No paywall, no ads, no agenda. Just receipts and the people willing to look at them.

How to Contribute

This project grows with the community. Here is how you can be part of it:

Flag an error. If a stat is wrong, a link is dead, or something does not match the source -- email me. I will check it and fix it. That is how this stays credible.

Submit a source. If you have data, a study, or a government report that belongs in one of these decks -- send it. All submissions are reviewed before anything gets added.

Share a deck. Put it in a group chat. Post it. Show it at a meeting. Print the stat. The most powerful thing you can do is put accurate information in front of someone who needs it.

Buy me a coffee. This project runs on time, research, and an AI subscription. If it has value to you, a coffee keeps the lights on. No pressure. No paywall either way.

Library Update April 2026

v1.0.3 — The Library Is Growing Up

Six new decks. A navigation overhaul. And a feature coming that changes what this project actually does for the people using it. Here’s everything that dropped, everything that changed, and everything coming next.

What’s Coming to the Library Itself

Every deck is getting an Agency Layer.

Here’s what that means: right now, you can read through any deck in this library, walk away with your mind blown and your blood pressure up, and have absolutely nowhere to put it. That was never the intention. Outrage with no outlet is just exhaustion wearing a different coat — and this community deserves better than that.

The Agency Layer closes every deck with something real. Not a hotline. Not a petition link that goes nowhere. Actual alternatives. Models that are working in real communities. Things you can do, support, or even just understand differently because of what you just read. The receipt was never meant to be the last word. It was always meant to be the first step.

This library was built from years of saying these things through poetry — through A Spoken Space, through every mic and every room where someone nodded because they already felt it but didn’t have the words. Now you have the data behind the feeling. The Agency Layer gives you somewhere to take it.

The research was always the poem before the poem. Now we’re writing the next verse together.

A community response feature is also coming — a simple, privacy-first way to submit your thoughts, local sightings, and reactions without creating an account or handing over data you didn’t mean to share. You submit. We read it. If it belongs in a future dispatch, it goes in — credited however you want, or anonymously. This library was never meant to be a monologue.

Under the Hood — What Changed

  • Static jump list → live search bar — type any topic, land on the deck instantly. On the index and the Source Bucket.
  • Back-to-top on every page — no more scroll-of-shame to get back to the nav
  • Source Bucket expanded — D29, D30, D32, D34, D38, D40 fully sourced and linked
  • Ordering and navigation standardized across every page and section
  • Updates page now runs on a rotating cycle — most recent work stays visible, what’s next stays honest, the archive lives permanently in the index where it belongs

What Just Dropped

D29 — National Security State.
$115 billion intel budget. 4.2 million Americans holding security clearances. Zero FISA surveillance applications denied by the court in all of 2024. The infrastructure of a surveillance state isn’t theoretical. It’s operational, it’s documented, and it’s in here.

D30 — The Truth About AI.
NVIDIA controls 87% of the AI accelerator market. OpenAI quietly removed the word “safely” from its mission statement. The U.S.-China AI benchmark gap closed from 17.5 points to 0.3 points in a single year. Everyone’s talking about AI. Almost nobody’s telling you who owns it, who it’s being used on, and who decided the rules.

D32 — The Unraveling.
17% of Americans report having zero close friends. Up from 3% in 1990. The local news is gone. The corner bar is a condo. The union hall is a parking lot. This deck is about what quietly collapsed — and the communities that are quietly rebuilding anyway.

D34 — The Federal Reserve Pt. 1.
Six bankers. A private island. Nine days in secret. November 1910. What came out of that meeting became the institution that controls the price of your mortgage, your car loan, your credit card, and your government’s debt. Part one of four.

D38 — American Farmers.
315 farm bankruptcies in 2025 — up 46% in one year. Four corporations control the majority of the seed supply. Bill Gates is now the largest private farmland owner in the country at 275,000 acres. The people growing this country’s food are being methodically priced, consolidated, and contracted out of existence.

D40 — Land, Air & Sea.
65% of active satellites belong to one private company. Undersea internet cables cut in the Baltic Sea — twice — in late 2024. Deep-sea mining fast-tracked by executive order over the objections of 930+ marine scientists. Who actually owns the physical infrastructure of the world? The answer is not governments.
