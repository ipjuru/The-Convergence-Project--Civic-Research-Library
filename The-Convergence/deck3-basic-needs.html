Chapter 1 — Food

47 Million Americans Don't Have Enough to Eat

Food insecurity in the United States has remained at crisis levels since the pandemic — and the cuts coming in 2025 are about to make it dramatically worse.

47.4M

People living in food-insecure households in 2023

13.7%

Of ALL U.S. households were food insecure in 2024 (USDA)

13.8M

Children in food-insecure homes — up from 13.5M the year before

The racial disparity is stark: 23.3% of Black households and 21.9% of Latino households experience food insecurity — more than double the rate of White non-Latino households (9.9%). This is not random. It is the accumulated weight of every broken policy, from redlining to SNAP cuts.

Sources: USDA ERS ERR-358 2024 · FRAC Hunger Report · Feeding America Elevating Voices 2025

Chapter 2 — The Policy Weapon

$187 Billion Ripped From the Hungry

H.R. 1, signed July 4, 2025, contains the largest SNAP cuts in the program's history. The Congressional Budget Office scored it. The numbers are not speculation.

Who loses: 4 million people lose food assistance or see it cut substantially. 800,000 adults 55–64. ~1 million in areas where jobs are scarce. 300,000+ veterans, homeless individuals, and former foster youth. All 40 million SNAP participants will see average benefits erode by ~$14/month by 2034 as Thrifty Food Plan adjustments are frozen. FRAC calculates H.R. 1 reduces income for the poorest 20% of Americans by 3.8% while increasing income for the wealthiest 20% by 3.7%.

Sources: CBO Score H.R. 1 · No Kid Hungry · CBPP · FRAC · Congress.gov R48552

Chapter 3 — The Price Tag

Food Prices Are Up 23.6% Since 2020

Food prices surged 23.6% from 2020 to 2024. Then came the tariffs. The households with the least absorb the most.

Tariff impact: The U.S. effective tariff rate peaked at 18.6% in August 2025 — highest since 1933. Food prices rose an additional 2.4–3.4% from tariffs alone. The average household lost $2,300–$2,400/year in purchasing power. The bottom income decile bears a burden 3× that of the top decile. 90% of Americans report being "stressed" about grocery costs (AP-NORC August 2025).

Sources: USDA ERS · BLS CPI · Yale Budget Lab Sept 2025 · AP-NORC Aug 2025

Chapter 4 — Shelter

You Need Six Figures to Buy a Home Now

The median home costs $416,900. You need to earn $106,000–$117,000 a year to afford it. The median household income is $82,200. The math doesn't work — and it wasn't an accident.

74.9% of U.S. households — 100.6 million people — cannot afford a median-priced new home (NAHB March 2025). The home price-to-income ratio hit 5.0, near all-time highs, up from 3.2 in the 1990s (Harvard JCHS). There is a shortage of 7.3 million affordable rental units. A typical household would need to spend 44.6% of income to afford the median home — nearly 15 points above the standard "affordable" threshold of 30%.

Sources: NAHB March 2025 · Harvard JCHS · NLIHC · Visual Capitalist · Realtor.com 2025

Chapter 5

Record Homelessness. Every Year.

771,480 people were homeless on a single night in January 2024 — the highest count ever recorded. Families with children surged 39.4%. And the cuts haven't fully landed yet.

150,000 children were homeless in 2024 — a 33% increase in one year. Older adults 55+ are the fastest-growing homeless population, projected to triple between 2017–2030. Family homelessness is now at its highest recorded level. This is not a housing shortage. This is a transfer of wealth — from people who need housing to corporations that treat it as an investment vehicle.

Sources: HUD Annual Homelessness Assessment Report Dec 2024 · National Alliance to End Homelessness 2025 Edition

Chapter 6 — Water

45% of Your Tap Water Has Detectable PFAS

The U.S. has some of the best water infrastructure in the world — and it's failing. Lead pipes, PFAS contamination, and infrastructure neglect affect communities of color hardest and most visibly.

4 million lead service lines remain in U.S. water systems (EPA 7th DWINSA). PFAS detected in 45% of tap water — contaminating ~7,500 locations affecting more than 130 million people (USGS). 2.2 million Americans live without running water or basic plumbing. 1 in 20 American Indian/Alaska Native households lack plumbing. Race is still "the strongest determinant" of water access gaps (NBC News / DigDeep). U.S. water infrastructure gets a grade of C− from the American Society of Civil Engineers.

Sources: EPA · USGS · DigDeep/US Water Alliance · ASCE 2025 Infrastructure Report Card

Chapter 7 — The Hidden Crisis

1 in 3 Households Can't Afford Their Electricity

Energy poverty is the crisis that connects the others. When you can't afford your electric bill, food spoils. The heat goes off. Medications that need refrigeration are compromised. Everything gets harder.

34 million households have reduced or forgone medical care to pay energy bills (Initiative for Energy Justice). Families below 50% of the poverty line spend 33% of income on energy. Black households face 64% higher energy cost burdens than White households in major metros. Average monthly electric bills rose from $121 in 2021 to $144 in 2024. Utility rates are projected to rise another 40% by 2030 — and AI data centers are a primary cause (Deck 1).

Sources: EIA · Initiative for Energy Justice · Science Advances · Health Affairs · Palmetto 2024

Chapter 8

The Compounding Effect Is the Point

None of these crises exist in isolation. They compound. When food, housing, water, and energy all fail at once, the body bears all of it — physiologically, not just economically.

The research is clear: Food insecurity + housing instability together create measurably worse health outcomes than either alone. Children in energy-insecure households have greater odds of hospitalization. Families spending 50%+ of income on shelter have no money left for food. H.R. 1's SNAP cuts + tariff-driven price increases + rising utility rates = a triple squeeze that reduces income for the poorest 20% by 3.8% while every essential cost climbs simultaneously.

Sources: FRAC · Urban Institute WBNS Dec 2025 · Health Affairs · CBS News Affordability Report Nov 2025

Chapter 9

Surviving and Building at the Same Time

You can't fix the system alone. But you can build resilience for yourself and your neighbors right now — without waiting for officials to act.

Food

Build a Food Network

  • Find or start a mutual aid food network in your zip code
  • Buy directly from local farmers — farmers markets, CSA boxes, co-ops
  • Community gardens create food AND neighborhood bonds
  • Know your SNAP rights — and who in your community is newly ineligible after H.R. 1

Housing

Know Your Protections

  • Research community land trusts in your area — they keep housing permanently affordable
  • Tenant unions have real power — organize with neighbors before crisis hits
  • Track corporate real estate purchases in your neighborhood (public record)
  • Push local government for community benefit agreements before any new development

Water & Energy

Decentralize

  • Get your tap water tested — EPA has free testing resources
  • Demand lead pipe replacement in your district — it's federally funded
  • Switch to an electric cooperative — nonprofits that serve members, not profit
  • Community solar programs lower bills and don't require ownership
