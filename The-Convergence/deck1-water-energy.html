Chapter 1

The Energy Appetite Is Doubling

Global data center electricity consumption hit 415 TWh in 2024. By 2030, it's projected to more than double — matching Japan's entire national power use. AI is the primary engine of this surge.

Goldman Sachs says data center power demand will surge 165% by 2030 vs. 2023. The U.S. alone goes from 180 TWh to 430 TWh — a 130% jump in six years.

Sources: IEA "Energy and AI" 2025 · Goldman Sachs · Lawrence Berkeley National Lab

Chapter 2

Your Electric Bill Is Going Up — Because of AI

Residential electricity rates rose 46% since 2019 and hit 19 cents/kWh nationally by end of 2025. In states with the highest data center density, rate hikes are far worse — and accelerating.

In Virginia — the world's data center capital — wholesale electricity costs rose up to 267% near data center clusters. Residential bills could rise 25–75% by decade's end. The PJM grid serving 67 million people across 13 states has already failed to procure sufficient capacity for the first time in its history.

Sources: EIA · NRDC · Jack Kemp Foundation · PJM Interconnection 2025

Chapter 3

They're Drinking the Water Too

A typical large data center uses 3–5 million gallons of water per day — equivalent to a city of 50,000 people. And they're building them in the driest places in the country.

Over 32% of U.S. data centers sit in high or extremely high water stress areas. Two-thirds of data centers built since 2022 are in drought-prone regions. U.S. data centers directly consumed 17 billion gallons in 2023 — plus 211 billion gallons indirectly through electricity generation.

Sources: Ceres "Drained by Data" · Bloomberg · Lawrence Berkeley National Lab · IEA

Chapter 4

Big Tech's Individual Water Tab

These companies disclose water use reluctantly. What they do reveal is staggering — and most of it evaporates, meaning it never returns to local supplies.

Google's Council Bluffs, Iowa facility alone consumed 1 billion gallons in one year — enough to supply all Iowa residential water for five days. Microsoft's water use rose 34% year-over-year, with 42% drawn from water-stressed areas.

Sources: Google Environmental Report · Microsoft ESG Report · Meta Sustainability Report · Amazon 2023

Chapter 5

$380 Billion Spent in One Year

In 2025 alone, the Big Four tech companies committed over $380 billion to infrastructure — mostly AI data centers. That's more than twice what the entire U.S. electric utility industry invests in a year.

For context: the U.S. electric utility industry invested approximately $160 billion in all generation, transmission, and distribution in 2024. Big Tech is outspending the energy sector on energy-adjacent infrastructure by a factor of 2.

Sources: CNBC · CreditSights · company Q4 2025 earnings filings

Chapter 6

The Grid Is Already Cracking

The PJM Interconnection's capacity auction prices — the mechanism that keeps the lights on for 67 million Americans — have skyrocketed in three years. The grid is not keeping up with demand.

By June 2027, PJM projects it may fall below reliability standards — meaning rolling blackouts during heat waves or storms are a real risk. PJM's own market monitor testified that this crisis is "almost entirely the result of large data center load additions."

Sources: PJM Interconnection · NRDC · Utility Dive · Belfer Center Feb 2026

Chapter 7

The Jobs Promise vs. Reality

States give away hundreds of millions in tax breaks to attract data centers. The permanent jobs rarely match the hype. The U.S. Chamber of Commerce's own data tells the story.

Meta's Louisiana data center — costing a potential $27 billion — will sit in a parish where 25% of people live below the poverty line. It needs electricity twice the peak output of New Orleans. Louisiana granted 20-year tax exemptions with a minimum requirement of just 50 jobs. Meanwhile, locals will pay for $3.2 billion in new gas power plants through their utility bills.

Sources: WIRED · Louisiana Illuminator · CNBC · U.S. Chamber of Commerce

Chapter 8

Who Pays for All of This?

The answer is you. Through utility bills. Through taxes. Through water you can no longer afford or access. The gap between what data centers cost communities and what they return is not accidental — it's the model.

PJM consumers face an estimated $100 billion in extra costs through 2033, translating to roughly $70/month extra per household by 2028. 7 qualifying Indiana data center projects may yield $150M to $900M in forgone state revenue — money that would have funded schools and roads. 25 of 31 Virginia communities with data centers had nondisclosure agreements with local officials before they knew what was coming.

Sources: NRDC · Mirror Indy · University of Mary Washington · CNBC

Chapter 9

What You Can Actually Do

Alarm without action is just anxiety. Here's where your power is real.

At Home

Lower Your Exposure

  • Switch to a local electric cooperative — they're nonprofits that return profits to members
  • Join or form community solar co-ops in your area
  • Push your utility company to disclose how much power goes to data centers
  • Collect rainwater where legal — it's a start

In Your Community

Organize

  • Attend city council meetings when data center projects are proposed — those rooms are empty and powerful
  • Demand environmental impact assessments include water and electricity projections
  • Support local journalism covering these deals
  • Connect with Food & Water Watch local chapters

Big Picture

Follow the Money

  • Track your state's data center tax incentive deals — they're public record
  • Support legislation requiring data centers to pay for grid upgrades they cause
  • Demand water disclosure requirements — companies hide this data
  • Push for community benefit agreements before approvals
