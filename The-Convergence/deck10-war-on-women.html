Section 01

The Wage Gap: Worse Than You Think — and Getting Worse

In 2024, women working full-time, year-round earned 80.9 cents per dollar earned by men — down from 82.7 cents in 2023 and 84 cents in 2022 (IWPR/Census). This is the largest two-year decline since the 1960s. Over a 40-year career, this gap amounts to approximately $542,800 in lost earnings per woman (AAUW).

The racial breakdown reveals the true scope. Compared to every dollar earned by white men: Asian women ~96 cents, white women ~83 cents, Native American women ~74 cents, Black women ~70 cents, and Latina women just 58 cents. At current rates, Black women's pay will not reach parity with white men's until 2227 (IWPR). The "motherhood penalty" is real: mothers earn 74 cents for every dollar earned by fathers, while fathers receive a roughly 15% wage bonus over childless men.

Section 02

Maternal Mortality: America's Singular Shame

The U.S. maternal mortality rate in 2023 was 18.6 deaths per 100,000 live births — comparable to Palestine and Chile, not peer nations (CDC/NCHS). Most comparison countries have rates below 5 per 100,000. The U.S. rate is triple that of Sweden, Japan, Germany, and France. America is one of only 7 countries worldwide where maternal mortality has significantly increased since 2000 (WHO).

Black women die at a rate of 50.3 per 100,000 — 3.5 times the white rate (14.5) and 4.5 times the Asian rate (10.7). Approximately 50% of maternal deaths are preventable. Mortality rates run 18–49% higher in non-Medicaid expansion states. The U.S. remains the only high-income country without universal healthcare, leaving 8 million women of reproductive age uninsured.

Section 03

Post-Dobbs: Measurable Health Consequences

Since the June 2022 Dobbs decision, 14 states enacted total abortion bans and 18 states ban or severely restrict abortion, affecting approximately 62.7 million women and girls (GEPI, January 2025). Combined studies estimate ban states experienced 22,000 additional births, 59 excess maternal deaths, and 478 excess infant deaths since Dobbs (Milbank Memorial Fund, 2025).

Texas provides the starkest case study: maternal mortality rose 56% in the first full year of the state's ban, with Texas's 2023 maternal mortality rate 155% higher than California's. By contrast, maternal mortality fell 21% in states that expanded abortion access. Nearly 1 in 5 patients now travels out of state for care (up from 1 in 10 in 2020), and 55% of OB/GYN practitioners in ban states report their ability to follow medical standards has been compromised.

Section 04

Violence Against Women: An American Epidemic

The scale of violence against women in the U.S. is an outlier among wealthy democracies. 1 in 3 women (35.6%) have experienced rape, physical violence, or stalking by an intimate partner (CDC NISVS). On a typical day, domestic violence hotlines receive over 20,000 calls. Nearly 3 women are killed by an intimate partner every day.

The firearms connection is critical: 92% of all women killed with guns in high-income countries were in the United States. An abuser's access to a gun increases femicide risk by 400–500%. Of every 1,000 sexual assaults, only 310 are reported, 50 lead to arrest, 28 to conviction, and 25 to incarceration. Effectively 98% of perpetrators face no criminal accountability (RAINN/BJS).

Section 05

Poverty, Childcare & the Single-Mother Trap

Single-mother families face a poverty rate of 31.3% — nearly 6 times the rate for married-couple families (5.5%) (Census, 2024). The 7.3 million single mothers in the U.S. earn a median income of approximately $39,120 compared to $125,980 for married couples. Center-based infant care costs average over 40% of state median income for single mothers, yet only 1 in 7 eligible families receives childcare subsidies. 36.8% of single-mother households experience food insecurity.

Section 06

Real, Substantial Progress

What Improved

  • Title IX: girls' sports participation up 1,000%+ since 1972
  • Women earn majority of degrees at every level (associate through doctoral)
  • Women are now majority of college-educated labor force (2024)
  • Fortune 500 female CEOs: 55 in 2025 (up from 2 in 1998)
  • Women-owned businesses: $2.7 trillion in annual revenue
  • 55 doctoral degrees per 100 men (up from 10 per 100 in 1970)
  • Legal protections: Equal Pay Act, VAWA, Lilly Ledbetter Act
  • Pay transparency laws spreading (22+ states, 2022–2025)

What Declined or Stalled

  • Pay gap widened 2022–2024 (largest decline since 1960s)
  • Maternal mortality rising (uniquely among wealthy nations)
  • Reproductive access reduced post-Dobbs (62.7M affected)
  • Eating disorders rising in teen girls (social media link)
  • Domestic violence conviction rate: effectively 2.5%
  • Single-mother poverty: 31.3% — 6x married-couple rate
  • Only 11% of Fortune 500 CEOs are women (2025)

🔗 The Bridge Deck

Men's wages stagnant. Women's pay gap widening. Childcare unaffordable for both. The War on Men and War on Women share the same structural root: productivity soared since 1979 (+59.7%) while typical worker pay barely moved (+15.8%). The question is not who has it worse. The question is who benefits when men and women fight each other instead of the system.
